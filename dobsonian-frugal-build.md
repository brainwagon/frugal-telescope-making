# Dobsonian Telescope — Frugal Materials & Substitutes
## by Mark VandeWettering

---

## Introduction

I'm Mark VandeWettering. I've been lucky enough to volunteer as an instructor at the Chabot Telescope Makers
workshop since the early 1990s, helping hundreds of people on their telescope-making journey. Now that I'm freshly
retired, I've been dedicating more of my time and energy to renewing my own enthusiasm for the craft — getting back
into building and revising some of my old projects, as well as helping others with theirs.

One thing I've noticed over the past three decades is that building your own telescope has gotten steadily more
expensive. These days you may well be paying a premium for the honor of spending dozens of hours (if not more)
building one yourself, rather than simply satisfying the urge through a little retail therapy.

Even so, it's an awesome hobby, and making things is always more rewarding than buying them. I actually don't
think of myself as an amateur astronomer: I'm an amateur telescope maker. I've spent far more time building
telescopes (and helping others build them) than using them. I recognize that makes me a bit strange, but it's
something I'm willing to live with.

Still, hobbies are better when they're affordable. Since I spend a lot of my time teaching others to build
telescopes, finding cheaper ways to do it makes the whole hobby more accessible. This little document grew out of
a desire to question my own methods and find more economical approaches. My goal isn't to make _cheap_ telescopes,
but to make good ones for less money — using less specialized equipment and more readily available materials, by
techniques that may differ from what you'll see in the usual literature.

One avenue I've been exploring (probably worth a document of its own) is 3D printing many of the common parts that
are otherwise surprisingly expensive. Requiring a 3D printer might seem like an added expense, but the printers
keep getting better and cheaper, and even if you don't own one, you can often have a part printed by a service for
less than a "real" part would cost.

There's plenty else that can be made for less, too. This list began when I was trying to figure out how to build
the rocker box bottom for a small 6" telescope I was reconstructing. The method "serious" makers usually recommend
is Wilsonart Ebony Star laminate — but it's typically a special-order item sold only in full 4'x8' sheets for $80
or so. I wanted a different solution, and especially one that would be more accessible to my students.

I've been playing with AI lately, so I decided to brainstorm ideas by talking through the problem with one. This
isn't meant to be a definitive reference; it's mostly just the product of an hour of brainstorming. It gave me a
few genuinely useful ideas for solving certain problems economically, and perhaps it will be of some use to you.

This work is entirely in the public domain.  Feel free to reproduce it and use it as you see fit.  No warranty
is implied or granted.  If you use some of these ideas and your telescope breaks in half, the only thing I can say
is that you get to keep both halves.

I hope you find something worthwhile.

## 1. Rocker Box Bottom (Ground Board Laminate)

**The usual recommendation:** Wilsonart "Ebony Star" laminate (4'×8' sheet, contact cement required). Only ~2' diameter circle needed — the rest is waste.

## 2. Teflon (PTFE) Pads

Dobsonians use PTFE pads in two places with different constraints:

- **Ground board pads** — 3 or 4 pads on the rocker box bottom, sliding against the ground board laminate. Can be thick, countersunk screws are fine.
- **Cradle (altitude bearing) pads** — on the rocker box sides, riding against the altitude trunnions. Must be thin (~1/8″ or less); screw heads can foul the bearing surface.

### Part A: Ground Board Pads (thick, screws OK)

The classic: 1/4″ virgin PTFE sheet, cut into ~1″ squares or circles, countersunk wood screws.

| # | Option | Cost | Thickness | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | **HDPE cutting board scraps** | ~$2–5 | 1/8″–3/8″ | Cut pads from a dollar-store cutting board. HDPE-on-laminate friction nearly as good as PTFE. Use a sharp countersink. |
| 2 | **UHMW furniture glide strips** | ~$5 | 1/16″–1/8″ | Sold as drawer-slide tape or stick-on strips. Stack 2–3 layers with VHB tape between to build thickness, then screw through. |
| 3 | **Plastic putty knives (nylon/PP)** | ~$1 | ~1/16″ | The blade of a cheap plastic putty knife yields several pad-sized pieces. Nylon or polypropylene. Countersink carefully. |
| 4 | **Nylon furniture glides (domed)** | ~$3/pack | ~3/16″ | Pre-made screw-on dome glides. Already have a nail hole. The dome shape gives a small contact area, which reduces stiction. Screw right on. |
| 5 | **PVC trim board scrap** | ~free | 1/4″–3/4″ | PVC lumber offcuts (e.g. AZEK or Veranda trim) sand smooth. Higher friction than PTFE but surprisingly usable, especially waxed. |

**Workshop pick:** HDPE cutting board pads — $2 yields enough for a dozen scopes, cuts easily with a wood saw, and you can pre-drill and countersink with standard wood bits.

### Part B: Cradle / Altitude Bearing Pads (thin, screw-free or hidden screws)

The classic: 1/8″ PTFE sheet, contact-cemented or epoxied to the rocker box. Specialty adhesive is the pain point.

| # | Option | Cost | Thickness | Attachment | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-----------|------------|-------|
| 1 | **UHMW adhesive tape ("slick tape")** | ~$5–8/roll | 0.5–1.5 mm | Self-adhesive | Sold as "drawer slide tape" or "furniture glide tape". Cut strips, peel, stick. No glue, no screws. Extremely low friction. |
| 2 | **Self-adhesive PTFE furniture glides** | ~$4/pack | ~2 mm | Self-adhesive | Pre-cut discs or rectangles from any hardware store. Already PTFE, already adhesive-backed. Just stick them on. |
| 3 | **Plastic report covers (polypropylene)** | ~$2 | ~0.5 mm | Contact cement or VHB tape | Smooth office-supply report covers. Stack 2–3 layers. Floppy but effective once stuck down. |
| 4 | **HDPE cutting board, countersunk screws from inside** | ~$2 | 1/8″ and up | Countersunk screws from underside | If the rocker box side wall is thin ply (1/2″), you can screw outward from *inside* the box so the screw head is buried in the wall and the pad is flush. Pre-drill from the outside first. |
| 5 | **Guitar picks (Delrin/nylon)** | ~$5/dozen | ~1 mm | Epoxy or super glue | Large triangle picks. Delrin and nylon are naturally slippery. Sand the flat back for glue adhesion. Fun "maker" aesthetic. |
| 6 | **Heavy-duty zip tie sections** | ~$1 | ~1.5 mm | Super glue + baking soda | Cut the flat section from ½″-wide heavy-duty zip ties. Rough the gluing side with sandpaper. Super glue + baking soda creates a strong fillet. |
| 7 | **PVC trim + VHB tape** | ~$3 | 1/4″ | VHB tape | Thin PVC lumber offcut, sanded smooth, attached with VHB foam tape. Removable but strong. Tape adds ~1 mm, so pick the thinnest PVC you can find. |
| 8 | **Aluminum soda can + epoxy** | ~free | ~0.1 mm | Epoxy or contact cement | Cut flat strips from soda cans. The aluminum is naturally low-friction against laminate when sanded smooth. Can layer to build thickness. Fragile, but zero cost. |

**Workshop pick:** UHMW adhesive tape for simplicity — no glue, no screws, just cut and stick. For a truly zero-cost option, report cover plastic + contact cement.

### Adhesives that work (no specialty contact cement)

| Adhesive | Works on | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| **3M VHB foam tape** | HDPE, UHMW, metals | Available everywhere as "heavy-duty mounting tape." Holds extremely well. Adds ~1 mm thickness. |
| **Super glue + baking soda** | Nylon, Delrin, hard plastics | The baking soda acts as an accelerant and gap-filler. Sand the plastic first for a mechanical key. |
| **Gorilla Glue (polyurethane)** | HDPE, UHMW (if roughed up) | Expands slightly. Clamp while curing. Requires sanding the plastic surface to 80-grit. |
| **Silicone caulk** | Most materials | Removable, flexible, cheap. Not as strong as VHB but good enough for light pads. Smear thinly. |
| **2-part epoxy (5-minute)** | Metals, nylon, roughed plastics | Universally available. Rough the plastic surface thoroughly. |
| **Hot glue** | Rough plywood-to-plastic | Temporary or permanent on porous surfaces. Not great on smooth plastic — but if the pad has a textured back, it'll hold. |

> **Key tip for any glue:** PTFE and HDPE are notoriously glue-resistant. Always sand the gluing surface with 60–80 grit sandpaper first to create a mechanical key. Without this, almost nothing sticks long-term.

## 3. Interior Tube Blackening

The inside of the OTA must be flat black to kill stray light. Any sheen = reflections across the focal plane = washed-out contrast.

**The classic:** Krylon Ultra Flat Black spray paint (#1602). Excellent, but ~$10–15/can, requires ventilation, and not every store stocks it.

### A. Spray Paint Alternatives

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **Rust-Oleum Camouflage Ultra Flat Black** | ~$7–9 | Walmart, auto parts stores, hardware stores. Camo paint must not reflect — intentionally dead-flat. Widely available and cheaper than Krylon UF. |
| 2 | **BBQ / Stove High-Heat Flat Black** | ~$8 | Any hardware store. Heat-resistant formulation has a dead-matte finish by nature. Slightly more durable than regular spray. |
| 3 | **MTN / Montana graffiti artist matte black** | ~$7 | Craft/art stores. Graffiti "ultra matte" cans have very high pigment load and a butyl acetate carrier that bites into cardboard well. Designed for zero shine. |
| 4 | **Generic chalkboard spray paint** | ~$6–8 | Any hardware store. By design it's the flattest finish they make. The slight tooth is actually beneficial for light scatter. |

**Spray pick:** Krylon Ultra Flat Black if you can find it. Rust-Oleum Camouflage is the best commonly-found substitute — cheaper and in more stores, but not as extensively vetted by the ATM community.

#### Spraying the Inside of a Tube: Technique Tips

Spraying a long enclosed cylinder is harder than it looks. Overspray bounces back, paint pools in the bottom, and it's easy to miss spots near the middle.

**Setup**
- Do it outdoors or in a *very* well-ventilated space. Respirator (N95 minimum, organic vapor preferred) — not a dust mask.
- Lay the tube horizontally on sawhorses or a scrap plywood V-block so you can rotate it easily.
- Mask off the ends you don't want painted (focuser hole, cell mounting areas, etc.). A rolled-up newspaper stuffed in each end catches overspray.
- Warm the can in a bucket of hot tap water for 5 min before spraying — improves atomization and helps the paint bite.

**Technique**
- **Light passes only.** The #1 mistake is trying to cover it in one go. 3–4 light passes with 10–15 min between will look far flatter than one heavy wet coat. Heavy coats create sheen.
- **Start at the far end, work back toward you.** Your arm is inside the tube. Point the nozzle away from yourself, toward the far wall. Work in sections — spray the top half, rotate 180°, spray again.
- **Use a "dusting" distance.** Hold the can 18–24″ away, not the usual 10–12″. You want a fine mist, not a wet blast. The overspray cloud inside the tube will settle into crevices you can't reach directly.
- **Rotate as you go.** Spray a short arc (~1/4 of the circumference), rotate, repeat. This prevents drips and ensures even coverage. A helper on the far end rotating slowly while you spray works beautifully.
- **For long tubes (f/8+), use a nozzle extension or "spray trigger."** A pistol-grip trigger attachment for spray cans (~$3–4) lets you hold the can deeper inside with better control. Some people tape the can to the end of a stick and actuate with a string — functional but fiddly.
- **Check with a flashlight at a grazing angle.** After it dries, shine a bright light across the surface almost parallel to the tube wall. Any gloss or missed spots will jump out immediately. Touch up those areas.

**Cardboard (Sonotube) specific**
- Cardboard absorbs the first pass unevenly. Don't panic — the second pass will even it out.
- Don't saturate. Over-wetting cardboard raises fibers and weakens the tube. Light, dry passes only.
- Consider sealing the inside with a thin coat of watered-down PVA glue (1:1 water:glue) first. It hardens the surface and makes the paint go further.

**Metal tube specific**
- Wipe down with isopropyl alcohol or acetone first to remove any oils.
- Satin vs. flat: on a smooth metal surface, spray "flat" can still show a slight sheen. Dusting the final coat from extra distance (24″+) while it's tacky will leave a micro-textured surface that scatters light better.

### B. Brush-On Options (no spray booth needed)

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **Black gesso (artist primer)** | ~$8–10 (pint) | Art/craft store. Acrylic gesso is extremely matte — it's calcium carbonate + pigment + acrylic binder. Brush-roll it on. Already dead-flat by design. Top pick for brush-on. |
| 2 | **Flat black latex sample jar** | ~$4 (8 oz) | Paint counter at any hardware store. Pick the cheapest flat black, get the sample size. Enough for a full tube. Add 1 tsp baking soda per 4 oz for texture. |

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |

**Lining pick:** Self-adhesive velvet flocking — zero cure time, no fumes, no cleanup, and arguably *better* optically than any paint surface.

## 4. Altitude Bearings (Trunnions)

The altitude bearings let the tube pivot smoothly on the rocker box. They need to be large enough to provide the right amount of friction — too small and the scope won't stay put when you point it, too large and it's hard to move.

**The classic:** Large semi-circular or full-circle plywood cutouts with Ebony Star laminate on the edge, riding on PTFE pads. For an 8" scope, you're looking at ~16–24" diameter bearing arcs.

The cost issues: laminating curved edges is fussy, Ebony Star again, and the bearing blanks waste plywood.

### A. Large Circular/Arc Bearings (traditional trunnion style)

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **Plywood arc + aluminum roof flashing** | ~$5 | Cut the plywood arc, wrap the edge with aluminum flashing strip (sold in rolls for roofing). Secure with small nails or staples. Very slippery against PTFE/HDPE pads. |
| 2 | **Plywood arc + Formica/laminate sample chips** | free | Big box stores give away laminate sample chips (~3″×5″). Butter the back of each chip with construction adhesive and clamp them end-to-end along the arc edge. Trim flush with a router or file. |
| 3 | **Plywood arc + PVC pipe split lengthwise** | ~$8 | A 3″ or 4″ PVC pipe coupler, split in half, epoxied to the plywood arc edge. The PVC is naturally slippery against plastic pads. One coupler yields two bearing surfaces. |
| 4 | **Plywood arc + vinyl cove base / baseboard** | ~$6 | Vinyl wall base (the flexible strip used where wall meets floor) is smooth and cheap. Glue along the bearing edge with contact cement. Comes in 4′+ lengths. |
| 5 | **Plywood arc + PET soda bottle strips** | ~free | Cut 2-liter soda bottles into 1½″-wide strips. The PET plastic is naturally very low friction. Attach with VHB tape or small flat-head nails along the arc. |
| 6 | **Plywood arc + bare, sealed, and waxed** | ~$2 | Sand the plywood edge smooth, seal with 3 coats of polyurethane, buff with paste wax. Works for small scopes (≤6″). Friction breaks in after a few sessions. |

**Arc bearing pick:** Aluminum flashing — cheap, conforms to the curve, no laminating skills needed, one roll does several scopes.

### B. Small Trunnions / Hub Bearings (no large arcs)

Instead of large arcs, use small cylindrical hubs that ride on the rocker box pads. Less material, easier to make.

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **PVC pipe caps (end plugs)** | ~$4/pair | Hardware store plumbing aisle. 3″ or 4″ PVC caps have a smooth, slightly domed exterior. Bolt through the center to the OTA sides. Excellent out of the box. |
| 2 | **Drain pipe test plugs** | ~$6/pair | Plumbing — mechanical test plugs with rubber expansion rings. The metal face is smooth. Bonus: you can adjust tilt by how far you tighten them. |
| 3 | **PVC pipe coupler (full-circle bearing)** | ~$5/pair | A section of 3″–4″ PVC coupler, epoxied into a plywood half-circle or triangle gusset. The coupler itself becomes the bearing surface. |
| 4 | **Furniture casters (wheel-style)** | ~$8/pair | Fixed casters (not swivel), mounted horizontally so the wheel rim becomes the trunnion. Already designed for smooth rolling. Looks a bit industrial but works. |
| 5 | **Hardwood dowel + PVC sleeve** | ~$4 | A 1″ hardwood dowel through the OTA, with a short PVC pipe sleeve over each end as the bearing surface. Simple, lightweight, good for 4.5″–6″ scopes. |
| 6 | **Toilet flange bolts + nylon washers** | ~$3 | For tabletop Dobsonians. A pair of ¼″ bolts through the tube with nylon washers stacked to ~1″ diameter. Tiny bearing area but functional at very small scale. |

**Small trunnion pick:** PVC end caps — one-step solution, no cutting, predrilled center for bolt, consistent diameter, available everywhere.

### C. Lazy Susan / Ring Bearing (alternative motion design)

Instead of trunnions, mount the OTA on a lazy Susan bearing ring — the whole tube rotates on a vertical-axis ring. This is a different motion design but eliminates altitude bearings entirely.

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **Lazy Susan bearing hardware** | ~$5–10 | Any hardware store. A square bearing plate that mounts between the OTA and a cradle. The tube stays balanced on-axis. Good for 6″–8″. |
| 2 | **HDPE ring cut from a bucket lid** | ~$2 | Cut a 10″–12″ ring from a 5-gallon bucket lid. Sand both faces smooth. Sandwiched between two plywood rings that bolt to the rocker box and OTA respectively. No moving parts — just two plastic surfaces. |
| 3 | **Two stacked Formica sample chips** | free | Two laminate chips, smooth face to smooth face, with a bolt through the center. A zero-cost lazy Susan. |

### D. Friction Adjustment (the real problem)

Whatever bearing material you pick, the hard part is getting the right *feel* — smooth motion with no stiction, and enough resistance that the scope stays pointed when you let go (even at low altitudes near the horizon).

**Cheap friction-tuning tricks:**

- **Teflon pad spacing:** Moving the rocker box pads closer together increases friction; moving them apart decreases it. No parts needed — just reposition the screws.
- **Spring-loaded tensioner:** A scrap of spring steel (from an old windshield wiper blade) pressing one pad against the bearing. Tighten/loosen with a single screw.
- **Felt washer on the trunnion bolt:** Between the OTA and rocker box side, add a felt washer. Compress it slightly with the nut. It adds a small, steady drag.
- **Magnetic dampening:** For metal bearings, glue a small rare-earth magnet to the rocker box near the trunnion. Adjust the gap for eddy-current drag. Silent, smooth, zero wear. Requires the bearing surface to be aluminum.

## 5. DIY Focuser

Commercial Crayford focusers run $80–200+. For a workshop scope, this is usually the single biggest cost saving you can make.

**The classic DIY:** A sliding friction tube inside a slightly larger tube, pushed/pulled by hand. Works — but coarse.

### A. Pipe-Nipple Friction Focuser (~$5)

The simplest functioning focuser for 1.25″ eyepieces.

**Parts:** 1½″ ABS or PVC slip-joint coupler, a short piece of 1¼″ thin-wall PVC (the inner drawtube), two #10 machine screws, and a scrap of Teflon or HDPE.

1. The 1¼″ PVC pipe slides inside the coupler. It's a near-perfect slip fit.
2. Drill and tap two holes in the coupler for #10 nylon thumbscrews. One holds the eyepiece, the other acts as a friction lock.
3. A strip of HDPE or UHMW tape inside the coupler smooths out the sliding action.
4. Mount the coupler to the OTA with a plywood base plate.

**Total cost:** ~$5 at any hardware store. 30 minutes to build.

### B. Threaded-Rod Helical Focuser (~$10)

A step up — uses a threaded rod to move the drawtube, giving finer control than friction-only.

**Parts:** 1¼″ PVC drawtube, 1½″ outer tube, ¼″-20 threaded rod (3″), matching wing nut or knob, spring, plywood bracket.

The threaded rod is fixed to the drawtube via a bracket. Turning the knob on the rod pushes/pulls the tube against a spring return. ~$10 in hardware.

### C. 3D-Printed Crayford (~$15 in filament + hardware)

If anyone in the workshop has access to a 3D printer, there are well-tested open-source Crayford designs (Thingiverse, Printables). The printed body houses the Teflon bearings and a roller shaft. Hardware is a few screws, a spring, and a piece of smooth rod for the drive shaft.

### D. Eyepiece Holders

Whatever the focuser design, you need to hold the eyepiece securely.

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **PVC threaded adapter + compression ring** | ~$3 | A 1½″ PVC male adapter with the threads on the inside — an eyepiece barrel is close to 1¼″ and a wrap of electrical tape makes a snug fit. Add a rubber O-ring inside as a compression ring. |
| 2 | **Copper pipe coupler** | ~$5 | A 1¼″ copper slip coupling is almost exactly the right ID for an eyepiece barrel. Solder a small brass nut over a drilled hole for a nylon thumb-lock screw. |
| 3 | **Film canister (for 1.25″)** | ~free | Some 35mm film canisters are a near-perfect fit for 1.25″ eyepieces. Cut off the bottom. Press-fit into the focuser drawtube. |

**Focuser workshop pick:** Pipe-nipple friction focuser. $5, 30 minutes, zero special tools, and it holds collimation well enough for visual use up to ~150×.

## 6. Primary Mirror Cell

Holds the mirror securely without pinching, allows collimation adjustment, and (for larger mirrors) provides edge support.

**The classic:** A plywood back plate with three collimation bolts + springs, mirror clips at the edges, and a sling or roller for edge support. Specialty springs and knurled knobs are often recommended.

### A. Springs & Collimation Hardware

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **Ballpoint pen springs** | free | Disposable click pens. Small, light springs — good for 4.5″–6″ mirrors. Stack two for more travel. |
| 2 | **Door latch spring (screen door)** | ~$2 | Hardware stores sell replacement springs for screen/storm door latches. Cut to length. Heavy enough for 8″–10″ mirrors. |
| 3 | **Rubber faucet washers instead of springs** | ~$2 | A stack of 3–4 rubber washers on each bolt provides progressive compression. No metal fatigue, no rust. The "no-spring" cell. |
| 4 | **Hardware store compression springs** | ~$3/pack | Ace/True Value usually have a spring assortment drawer. Look for ~3/4″ long, fits over a ¼″ bolt. |
| 5 | **Wing nuts instead of knurled knobs** | ~$2/pack | Wing nuts at the hardware store. Add a nylon washer under the wing nut for smoother turning. No specialty knobs needed. |
| 6 | **Plastic bottle caps as thumb knobs** | free | Epoxy a bolt head into a bottle cap (Gatorade caps are good). Instant knurled knob with good grip. |

**Spring pick:** Rubber faucet washers — no corrosion, consistent tension, available everywhere, no cutting required.

### B: Mirror Clips & Edge Support

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **Nylon furniture glides as mirror clips** | ~$2 | The same domed screw-on glides from the Teflon chapter. The nylon tip won't scratch the mirror. Pre-drilled for screws. |
| 2 | **Corner braces with rubber feet** | ~$3 | Small L-brackets from the hardware store with a self-adhesive rubber bumper on the mirror-contact face. |
| 3 | **Zip-tie mirror clamps** | ~$1 | Drill two small holes flanking the mirror position, thread a zip tie through, leave it loose enough to not pinch. Good for tabletop scopes. |
| 4 | **Webbing strap sling (edge support for ≥8″)** | ~$3 | Nylon webbing (from camping/backpacking section) — a 1″ strap with one end screwed to each side of the cell. The mirror rests in the U-shaped hammock. Better than any rigid edge contact. |
| 5 | **Old leather belt sling** | ~free | Same principle as webbing. An old belt, cut to length. Softer than nylon, won't scratch. |

**Edge support pick:** Nylon webbing sling — $3 at any outdoor/hardware store, proper engineering, works for any scope 8″+.

### C. The Whole Cell: Frugal Build Recipe

For a typical 8″ f/6:

1. **Back plate:** 3/4″ plywood circle, ~1″ larger than the mirror.
2. **3 support points:** 1/4″-20 carriage bolts from underneath, head countersunk into the plywood.
3. **Springs:** 3 stacks of 3 rubber faucet washers each, slipped over the bolts.
4. **Adjustment nuts:** 3 wing nuts + nylon washers on top.
5. **Mirror pads:** 3 small squares of cork or adhesive felt on the bolt heads (mirror rests on these, not on bare metal).
6. **Edge support:** A nylon webbing sling across the bottom third of the mirror.
7. **Mirror clips:** 3 nylon furniture glides, screw-mounted, with ~1/32″ gap above the mirror surface (paper-thickness clearance).

**Total cost:** ~$15. Needs only a drill, a saw, and a screwdriver.

## 7. Spider & Secondary Holder

The spider holds the secondary mirror at 45° in the center of the tube, with as little obstruction and diffraction as possible.

**The classic:** A four-vane steel spider with a machined aluminum secondary hub. $40–60 commercially.

### A. Spider Vane Materials

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **Music wire (spring steel)** | ~$3 | Hobby/model shop. 0.020″–0.030″ diameter for 6″–8″ scopes. Four lengths, tensioned by small turnbuckles or Z-bends. Near-zero diffraction. |
| 2 | **Guitar string (plain steel, .013–.017)** | ~$2 | Music store. Single strings. High-carbon steel holds tension well. Thin = minimal diffraction. |
| 3 | **Sewing pins pulled taut** | ~$1 | For tabletop/small scopes. 2″ dressmaker pins with the heads cut off, pulled tight. |
| 4 | **Hacksaw blade (flat metal vane)** | ~$3 | Cut a new hacksaw blade into two strips for a 2-vane curved spider. Spring steel, pre-drilled holes at the ends. Stiff and cheap. |
| 5 | **Aluminum yardstick strips** | ~$3 | Cut a cheap aluminum yardstick into 3/4″-wide strips. Drill ends for mounting bolts. Less stiff than steel but adequate for smaller scopes. |
| 6 | **Wooden dowels (3-vane)** | ~$2 | Three 1/8″ dowels at 120°. Thicker than wire = more diffraction, but rigid enough that you don't need tensioning. Paint flat black. |
| 7 | **Bicycle spokes** | ~$2 (bike shop) | Stainless steel, threaded on one end, perfectly straight, very high tension. One spoke = one vane. Use the factory nipple as a tensioner. |

**Vane pick:** Bicycle spokes — pre-threaded, stainless, tensionable with the included nipple, free from any bike shop's scrap bin.

### B. Secondary Hub (holds the diagonal mirror at 45°)

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **PVC pipe cap** | ~$2 | A 1″ or 1¼″ PVC end cap. The flat end is already at 90° to the tube axis. Cut a 45° wedge off to mount the mirror, or use a wooden wedge glued to the face. |
| 2 | **Wooden dowel, cut at 45°** | ~$1 | A 1″ hardwood dowel, sawed at 45° on one end. Epoxy the secondary directly to the angled face. Simple, rigid, adjustable by rotating the whole spider. |
| 3 | **Threaded rod + nut + fender washer** | ~$3 | 1/4″-20 threaded rod through the center of the secondary, with a rubber washer and a wing nut for tilt adjustment. Very adjustable, a bit crude-looking. |
| 4 | **Electrical conduit nut / lock ring** | ~$2 | The thin stamped metal nuts from electrical fittings. The right size makes a good mirror cell rim. Soft enough to bend for tilt adjustments. |

**Hub pick:** Hardwood dowel cut at 45° — simplest possible, near-zero cost, and lets you fine-tune rotation by turning the whole spider before locking it down.

### C. Secondary Mirror Adhesive

For attaching the secondary mirror to the hub, silicone caulk is the ATM standard (allows removal, doesn't stress the glass). A $4 tube of clear silicone from any hardware store does the job. Apply 3 small dabs, not a full bead — allows the glass to expand/contract.

## 9. Collimation Tools

You need a basic set to align the optics. A laser collimator is $30–60; a Cheshire $30–50.

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **Film canister collimation cap** | ~free | Classic DIY. Drill a 1–2 mm hole exactly in the center of a 35mm film canister cap. Place on the focuser. Your eye centered behind the hole. Works surprisingly well. |
| 2 | **Plumbing Cheshire** | ~5 | A short piece of chrome-plated brass sink drain pipe (1¼″), cut at 45° inside a PVC body. The shiny interior acts as the reflective surface. Add a crosshair at one end. |
| 3 | **LED + battery DIY cheshire** | ~$6 | Add a red LED and watch battery to the plumbing Cheshire for illuminated reticle. Even easier to see the doughnut. |
| 4 | **Reflective tape donut** | ~$2 | Instead of a Cheshire, put a ring of reflective tape or a binder-paper reinforcement ring on the primary mirror center for the collimation cap to target. |

**Collimation pick:** Film canister cap + binder-paper reinforcement ring on the primary. Total cost ~$0.50, effective for f/6 and slower.

## 10. The Tube (OTA)

The optical tube assembly holds the primary mirror at one end and the focuser/spider/secondary at the other, at a fixed distance, while keeping everything aligned. It's the single biggest structural component.

**The classic:** Sonotube (spiral-wound cardboard concrete form). Strong, lightweight, damps vibrations well, easily cut with hand tools. An 8″ scope uses 10″ diameter Sonotube — but it's not always in stock, and you're buying a 4′ section even if you only need 30″.

### A. Cardboard/Sonotube Options

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **Sonotube (concrete form)** | ~$15–25 (4′ section) | The standard. Check real hardware stores (not big box — they often only stock 8″ and 12″). 10″ and 14″ may require a specialty concrete supply. Call first. |
| 2 | **Sonotube competitor brands** | ~$10–20 | Quik-Tube, Sakrete Tube, TubeBase. Same product, different label. Any "concrete forming tube" works. |
| 3 | **Carpet roll tube** | free–$5 | Carpet stores and flooring warehouses throw away thick-walled 12″+ cardboard tubes. Call and ask — they may save one for you. Thicker wall than Sonotube (heavier but stiffer). |
| 4 | **Heavy-duty shipping tube** | ~$10–15 | Office supply or packaging store. 10″–12″ diameter, 36–48″ long. Thinner wall than Sonotube but adequate for 6″–8″ scopes. |
| 5 | **Fabric store bolt tube** | free–$5 | Joann, Michaels, fabric stores. Large fabric bolts ship on heavy cardboard tubes. Usually 2″–3″ diameter (good for finders or tabletop scopes) but occasionally wider. |
| 6 | **Fiberglass septic tank riser** | ~$20–30 | Specialty but interesting — a short length of fiberglass riser from a septic supply. Waterproof, rot-proof, needs no painting. Heavy. |

**Cardboard pick:** Sonotube if available, carpet roll tube if not — free for the asking, very thick wall.

### B. Solid (Non-Tubular) Designs — Wooden Box

A square or octagonal plywood box instead of a round tube. Easier to mount hardware on flat surfaces, uses only plywood.

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **Square plywood box** | ~$15–20 | 4 rectangles of 1/2″ plywood, butt-jointed. The square cross-section is wider than a tube but makes focuser and spider mounting trivial. Add triangular corner blocks inside for stiffness. |
| 2 | **Open-back box (3-sided)** | ~$10 | Three sides closed, the bottom side open (or mostly open). Lighter, still rigid. Good for the upper half of a truss scope. |
| 3 | **Octagonal box** | ~$20–25 | 8 narrow strips of 1/4″ plywood, beveled edges, taped and glued. Lighter than square, closer to a cylinder. Faner to make. |
| 4 | **Hardboard (Masonite) rolled tube** | ~$8 | Two layers of 1/8″ tempered hardboard, soaked in water, rolled around a form, glued and clamped until dry. Makes a custom-diameter tube for pennies. Takes practice. |

**Box pick:** Square plywood box — flat faces are a gift for mounting hardware, and it builds with a circular saw and glue. No tube-hunting required.

### C. Truss / Open-Frame Designs

Instead of a solid tube, use a truss: a rigid primary box ("mirror box"), a lightweight upper cage, and 4–8 poles or struts between them. Disassembles for transport. Saves weight. Looks impressive.

**The classic:** Aluminum tubes with machined connectors. $80+ in hardware alone.

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **Wooden dowels** | ~$8 | 3/4″–1″ hardwood dowels (oak or maple, not pine). 6 or 8 struts. Seat them in holes drilled into the mirror box and upper ring. Secure with a cotter pin or a small wedge. |
| 2 | **Aluminum tent / awning poles** | ~$10–15 | Replacement poles from camping stores or thrift-shop tents. Shock-corded sections can be separated. Very light, stiff, and pre-anodized. |
| 3 | **Electrical conduit (EMT)** | ~$10 | 1/2″ thin-wall EMT conduit (Home Depot). Cut to length, flatten the ends in a vise, drill a bolt hole. Stiff and cheap. Heavier than aluminum but dead rigid. |
| 4 | **PVC pipe** | ~$6 | 3/4″ or 1″ Schedule 40 PVC. Stiff enough for shorter trusses (6″–8″ scopes). Cuts with a hacksaw. Paint it flat black inside and out. |
| 5 | **Broom / mop handles** | ~$3 each | Wood or aluminum handles from the cleaning aisle. Typically ~48″ and straight. Cut to length. Pre-finished. |
| 6 | **Fiberglass driveway markers** | ~$2 each | 48″ orange fiberglass rods from hardware/winter section. Lightweight, stiff, won't rust. Four or six make a minimal truss for a travel scope. |
| 7 | **Bamboo garden stakes** | ~$1 each | Surprisingly stiff for the weight. 4′–6′ lengths, ~3/4″ diameter. Look great with shellac. Tie the ends with string and glue for a traditional build. |

**Truss pick:** Electrical conduit (EMT) — $10, extremely stiff, available everywhere, flat ends drill easily for bolt holes.

### D. Quick Tube Selector

| Scope size | Best cheap choice | Why |
|------------|-------------------|-----|
| 4.5″–6″ tabletop | Shipping tube or carpet roll | Small diameter, short, easy to find |
| 8″ f/6 | Sonotube 10″ (or square ply box) | Classic for a reason; ply box if Sonotube is unavailable |
| 10″ f/5 | 12″ Sonotube or 6-pole EMT truss | At this size, a truss starts making transport sense |
| 12″+ | Truss only | A solid tube at this aperture is cumbersome; truss from the start |

### E. Tube Liners & Light Baffles

If the tube interior is too shiny or the tube is oversized (common: 10″ tube for an 8″ mirror), add baffles.

| # | Option | Cost | Notes |
|---|--------|------|-------|
| 1 | **Cardboard ring baffles** | free | Cut rings from the tube offcut, paint flat black, glue inside at intervals. Step the inner diameter so no stray light can walk down the tube. |
| 2 | **Yogurt container baffle** | free | For small scopes. Cut the bottom off a large yogurt container, paint flat black, friction-fit inside the tube. |
| 3 | **Foam board baffle rings** | ~$3 | Dollar-store foam board, cut into rings with a utility knife. Lightweight, easy to paint. |

> **Tube diameter rule of thumb:** The tube ID should be at least the mirror diameter + 2″ (so an 8″ mirror needs ≥10″ tube). This keeps the tube wall out of the light path and allows some airflow. Oversizing further doesn't hurt optically but adds weight.

## 11. 3D-Printed Components

A 3D printer (even a basic $200 FDM printer) opens up precision parts that would otherwise require machining or expensive commercial components. Many libraries and makerspaces now have printers available for public use at near-zero cost. If one person in the workshop has a printer, they can produce parts for everyone.

**Filament notes:** PLA is fine for everything listed here — no need for ABS, PETG, or exotic materials. PLA is rigid enough, easy to print, and the black/dark colors are already low-reflectivity. Parts should be printed at 3–4 perimeters and 20–30% infill for structural stiffness.

### A. What's Worth Printing

| # | Component | Replaces | Filament cost | Notes |
|---|-----------|----------|---------------|-------|
| 1 | **Crayford focuser** | $80–200 commercial focuser | ~$3 | Well-proven open-source designs (Thingiverse, Printables). Needs ~$3 in hardware: 2 bearings, a spring, a piece of 8 mm smooth rod for the drive shaft, and a few M3/M4 bolts. Printed body houses the roller assembly and Teflon bearing pads. The 1.25″ eyepiece holder can also be printed. |
| 2 | **Primary mirror cell** | Plywood cell with springs & clips | ~$5 | Printed triangles/rocker arms for a 6-point or 9-point floating cell. PLA is stiff enough for 8″–10″ mirrors. No springs if using a compliant printed flexure design. Still needs 3 bolts + wing nuts for collimation. Print-in-place hinges eliminate assembling multiple pieces. |
| 3 | **Spider & secondary hub** | $40–60 machined spider | ~$2 | Central hub with integral 45° secondary mount, plus printed vane anchors. Vanes can be bicycle spokes, music wire, or printed-in-place thin strips. Split-hub designs let you rotate the secondary for alignment. Include a printed ring to fit the tube ID exactly and center the spider perfectly. |
| 4 | **Altitude bearings** | Large plywood arcs with laminate | ~$4 | Curved bearing arcs that bolt to the OTA sides. Can be printed in sections and bolted together for diameters beyond the print bed. Smooth printed PLA against PTFE/HDPE pads gives good friction. Include an integral mounting flange. |
| 5 | **Eyepiece tray / accessory holder** | none (pure convenience) | ~$2 | Eyepiece racks that bolt to the rocker box side. Holds 3–5 eyepieces + a collimation tool. Keeps everything at the eyepiece end and adds counterweight low on the scope. |
| 6 | **Rocker box corner brackets** | Metal L-brackets or joinery | ~$1 | Right-angle corner brackets with screw holes. Align the rocker box panels at exactly 90°. Faster and more repeatable than butt joints or pocket screws. |
| 7 | **Focuser mounting plate (curved)** | Hand-carved wooden block | ~$1 | A saddle that matches the tube radius on one side and has a flat focuser mount on the other. Solves the "mount a flat focuser on a round tube" problem perfectly. |
| 8 | **Collimation cap / Cheshire body** | Film canister or $40 Cheshire | ~$1 | A precise collimation cap with a centered 1–2 mm peephole. Print-in-place Cheshire with a slot for a 45° reflective insert (piece of chrome tape or a cut mirror fragment). |
| 9 | **Tube rings / truss connectors** | Aluminum tube rings ($30/pair) | ~$3 | Split rings for clamping round tubes, or truss-pole seats that bolt to the mirror box and upper cage. Threaded inserts heat-set into the print for machine-screw clamping. |
| 10 | **Finder mount / ring set** | Finder rings ($20–30) | ~$1 | Two split rings sized to a finder scope or red dot base, with an integral dovetail foot that matches the OTA mounting plate. Three-point adjustment with nylon screws for alignment. |
| 11 | **Secondary heater ring** | Dew prevention ($30+) | ~$2 | A thin ring that mounts behind the secondary mirror, with a groove for a resistor or nichrome wire. Keeps dew off the diagonal. Needs a battery pack and a resistor (~$2 more). |
| 12 | **Knobs & thumbscrews** | Knurled aluminum knobs ($5 each) | ~$0.10 | Replace wing nuts and hex bolts with printed knobs. Big, comfortable knobs for collimation bolts, focuser lock, and altitude tension. Print a hex or square recess to capture a nut. |

### C. When *Not* to 3D Print

Some things are better left to traditional materials:

- **Structural rocker box panels** — plywood is cheaper per square inch, stiffer, and faster than printing a 20″ part.
- **The OTA tube itself** — a printed tube large enough for an 8″ scope is impractical. Stick with Sonotube, a plywood box, or a truss.
- **Large bearing arcs** (for scopes 10″+) — plywood is lighter and cheaper at large scales. Print connectors and use plywood for the arcs.
- **The rocker box ground board** — melamine shelf board is already perfect and can't be improved with printing.
- **Mirror blanks** — obviously.

### D. Printing at a Library or Makerspace

If no workshop participant owns a printer:

- **Public libraries** — many now have 3D printers, some free, some at ~$0.10/gram. Larger metro libraries almost certainly do.
- **Makerspaces** — usually have a monthly membership (~$25–50) or a day-pass. Worth it if you're printing all the parts for several scopes.
- **High schools / community colleges** — instructors can often get access. A student in the workshop may have access through their school.
- **Online print services** — Craftcloud, Shapeways — expensive per part but reasonable for just a spider and focuser (~$20–30 shipped for everything). Compare to the $200+ in commercial parts this replaces.

> **Workshop tip:** If printing is available, the three highest-impact prints are the **focuser**, **spider/secondary hub**, and **altitude bearings**. Together they replace ~$160–$260 in commercial parts for ~$10 in filament and hardware.
